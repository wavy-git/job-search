## Core stories (ready to use)

### 1. $2M vendor analysis reframing (National Grid)

**Context**: Built defect tracking system across all AMI teams' QA operations (not just own team). Analyzed vendor quality failures comprehensively. Translated technical issues into financial impact. Showed leadership that fixing one single issue could prevent $2M+/month in losses from having to send technicians manually.

**Result**: Reframed vendor accountability from technical escalations to financial decisions executives could act on. Changed how the organization prioritizes vendor issues.

**Use for**: executive influence, analytics translation, stakeholder management, operational turnaround questions.

### 2. Log4Shell crisis response (Comcast, December 2021)

**Context**: CISA called Log4Shell "one of the most serious" cybersecurity vulnerabilities ever. Hundreds of millions of devices affected. Maz was NOT assigned to the crisis response. He saw the need, walked into the taskforce, offered his help, and proactively built crisis response analytics.

**Result**: His dashboard became the operational backbone that enabled the taskforce to identify, track, and fix vulnerabilities organization-wide.

**Use for**: initiative, operator pattern, seeing gaps others miss, crisis leadership, analytics rigor.

### 3. NCC revival (Infotech Director, 20 to 5,000 units)

**Context**: NCC = Network Communication Concentrator (payment transaction router). Obsolete Ingenico product, only 20 units deployed, considered dead. Maz negotiated directly with Ingenico leadership to secure software ownership while Ingenico retained hardware. Rebuilt the products with value-added capabilities: gas station distribution processing, smart card processing systems, transaction monitoring, blacklist/whitelist management.

**Result**: Grew from 20 units to 5,000+ (250x). No competitor could replicate because they didn't own the source code.

**Use for**: strategic thinking, partnership negotiation, building what others abandoned, creative reframing.

### 4. Multi-bank terminal (Infotech VP)

**Context**: Market's first bank-independent terminal. Merchants using accounts in multiple banks previously needed separate terminals per bank. Maz created one device consolidating all accounts.

**Result**: Three strategic outcomes from one product decision: (a) attracted merchants (convenience), (b) multiplied transaction volume (per-terminal density 20 to 90), (c) made every competitor terminal obsolete.

**Use for**: product strategy, customer-centric thinking, competitive positioning, payments expertise.

### 5. Central bank seat (Infotech VP)

**Context**: Co-architected the national card network at the central bank's table. Held a FORMAL seat, one of the key decision-makers. Built domestic payment switching infrastructure similar to VisaNet or Banknet. Unified transaction routing, authorization, settlement, security frameworks. Included PCI-DSS standards. Covered 30+ financial institutions.

**Result**: 99.5/100 score against new standards (compliance, quality, performance). Became nationally regulated.

**Use for**: industry-shaping work, gravitas, regulatory expertise, payments credibility at network scale.

### 6. Cloud platform delivery (Comcast)

**Context**: Data center network portion of Comcast's strategic in-house cloud platform (routing, switching, capacity expansion). Parallel initiatives assumed resources would have to be stolen from them.

**Result**: Delivered in 6 months against 12-month estimate. Zero impact on parallel initiatives.

**Use for**: delivery speed, resource efficiency, complex program execution.

### 7. Configuration-driven platform (Infotech Manager)

**Context**: Market's first configuration-driven payment application platform. Instead of hardcoded applications requiring weeks to build, Maz made code configurable.

**Result**: Release cycles dropped from 6-8 weeks to 3-5 days. Terminal deployments dropped from 5-6 months to 2-3 weeks. Customer base 2 to 8 major FIs. Terminal network 10K to 100K+. "The market was in shock."

**Use for**: technical innovation, market-making, speed as strategy, early-career credibility.

### 8. VP Infotech turnaround (2013-2015)

**Context**: Company was in bad shape. Toxic culture, trust at lowest, customers (banks) losing confidence, losing contracts, not expanding, bleeding money. Critical 60K terminal contract with only 10K deployed.

**Result**: 10K to 120K merchants, 150M+ annual transactions, 54x volume growth within 18 months. Four first-to-market innovations: predictive merchant targeting, pull-based logistics, automated remote configuration, multi-bank terminal.

**Use for**: turnaround experience, scaling under pressure, operational leadership, payments at merchant scale.

## Anticipated interview questions

### "Why VP to TPM?" (if asked about career trajectory)

"Deliberate move. I left a VP role to relocate to the US, earn an MBA, and enter the American market. Comcast wasn't a step down, it was a strategic entry point into Fortune 50 enterprise scale. The outcomes prove it: tripled deployment throughput, built analytics that powered a national cybersecurity crisis response, and was recruited to replicate my model across a larger division."

### "Do you have direct reports?" (at NG)

"At National Grid, I lead through platform ownership. 30 people work on my platform day to day. Eight teams deliver on my platform. It's leadership through influence, not org chart authority. My earlier roles at Infotech prove I can build and manage large direct teams (12 direct reports, 50+ employees, 700+ field technicians). At NG, the challenge is different: making an entire program succeed through the platform I own."

### Standard behavioral expectations

- Tell me about a time you influenced without authority → $2M vendor analysis or Log4Shell
- Tell me about a time you took initiative → Log4Shell or CCRE change management
- Tell me about a strategic product decision → NCC revival or multi-bank terminal
- Tell me about a cross-functional program → NG AMI platform or Infotech national card network
- Tell me about a turnaround → Infotech VP or Comcast deployment operations

### "What's a product you wish you built?" (reusable)

A technology that captures, stores, and transmits scent. Smell is the only sense that bypasses the brain's processing centers and goes straight to memory and emotion. It's why the smell of a specific bakery can pull you back twenty years before you've even named the feeling. And yet every digital product we've ever built is silent on this entire dimension. Sound, sight, sometimes touch, but never smell. There's a whole language of presence we haven't been able to write.

I want to build the infrastructure that closes that gap. Imagine a photo of yourself as a child in the kitchen returning the scent of the cookies your grandmother was baking that evening. A video call with someone overseas carrying the smell of their morning coffee. A history lesson about an ancient marketplace landing the spice and smoke of the actual place. A horror game where the sewer you're hiding in smells like a sewer. Once it works, the question isn't what we'd use it for. It's what we couldn't. It would add a dimension to digital experience that has never existed before, and the parts of the brain it would reach are the ones that make us feel something is real.

**Use for**: any "product you wish you built" or "favorite product idea" or "blue-sky problem" question. Shows product imagination, taste, range, and humanity.

## Voice and brand

### Core beliefs to express

- "The way most problems stay problems is that people stop questioning them."
- The gap between exceptional and average teams is imagination, not ambition.
- Leadership: understand what's actually happening vs. what everyone assumes, build clarity, make decisions visible, reduce friction.
- Technology should serve people, not constrain them.

### Pattern to demonstrate

Every role follows the same pattern: enter a situation where something is broken or missing, question what everyone else accepts, build something better.

### Not to sound like

- Not a consultant (translating strategy, driving outcomes, measurable impact)
- Not a generic product leader (outcomes, roadmaps, alignment)
- Not apologetic about non-traditional path (VP to TPM is a strategic pivot, not a step back)
